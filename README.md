# Overview
This Master's dissertation investigates a fundamental question in virtual reality design for mental well-being: how does the type of audio affect user experience in a nature-based mindfulness VR application?

While VR shows promise for supporting mindfulness and stress reduction, most research focuses on visual design. The specific impact of auditory elements remains underexplored. This study provides empirical evidence comparing two prevalent audio types:

| Audio Condition |	Description |	Type |
| --------------- | ----------- | ----- |
| Nature Sounds |	Diegetic soundscape (birdsong, flowing water, wind) |	Environmentally congruent |
| Mindfulness Audio	| Non-diegetic guided meditation + 432Hz ambient soundtrack	| Structured practice |

Key finding: Both audio types effectively reduce negative affect (stress, distress), but diegetic nature sounds foster a significantly stronger sense of presence – participants felt genuinely "there" in the virtual forest, whereas the mindfulness audio was perceived as a detached layer.

# Methodology
Experimental Design
| Aspect | Detail |
| --------------- | ----------- |
| Design | Mixed-methods, within-subjects, counterbalanced |
| Participants | N = 26 (adults 18+, screened for VR safety) |
| VR Hardware | Meta Quest 2 (standalone) |
| Development Platform | Unity 2020.3.36 LTS |
| VR Environment | Photorealistic forest scene (static viewpoint) |
| Condition Duration | 12 minutes per condition |

Measures
| Instrument | Purpose | When Administered |
| --------------- | ----------- | ---- |
| PANAS (Positive and Negative Affect Schedule | Emotional state (20 items) | Pre-VR baseline + post-condition |
| IPQ (Igroup Presence Questionnaire) | Sense of presence (14 items, 4 subscales) | Post-condition |
| Semi-structured interviews | Qualitative insights, preferences | Post-study |

# Key Results

Emotional Response (PANAS)
| Affect Type | Condition | Baseline Mean (SD) | Post-VR Mean (SD) | Mean Change | p-value | Effect Size (Cohen's d) |
| --------------- | ----------- | ---- | -- | -- | --- | --- |
| Negative Affect | Nature Sounds | 17.5 (6.4) | 13.0 (5.2) | -4.5 | 0.002 | 0.687 |
| Negative Affect | Mindfulness | 17.5 (6.4) | 13.0 (4.9) | -4.5 | 0.001 | 0.730 |
| Positive Affect | Nature Sounds | 31.6 (6.1) | 30.7 (6.9) | -0.9 | 0.497 | 0.135 |
| Positive Affect | Mindfulness | 31.6 (6.1) | 30.5 (7.7) | -1.1 | 0.352 | 0.186 |

Conclusion: Both audio types significantly reduce negative affect (medium-large effect sizes). Neither significantly increases positive affect in short-term exposure.

Sense of Presence (IPQ)
| IPQ Subscale | Nature Sounds (M) | Mindfulness Audio (M) | p-value | Effect Size (Cohen's d) |
| --------------- | ----------- | ---- | -- | -- |
| Spatial Presence | 3.69 | 1.19 | 0.047 | 0.41 | 
| Involvement | 3.35 | 0.27 | 0.002 | 0.68 | 
| Experienced Realism | 3.23 | 0.81 | 0.004 | 0.62 | 
| General Presence | 2.62 | 1.08 | 0.011 | 0.54 |

Conclusion: Nature sounds significantly outperform mindfulness audio across all presence dimensions, with the largest effect on psychological Involvement.

Qualitative Themes (Thematic Analysis)
| Theme | Key Quote | Interpretation |
| --------------- | ----------- | ----- |
| Immersion through authentic sound | "[Nature Sounds] felt more immersive and felt more real... felt like it was all part of the same thing." (P9) | Diegetic sound creates a cohesive, believable world |
| Detaching effect of incongruent audio | "[With Mindfulness Audio] it actually felt like almost like I was sat there, but had my headphones in." (P5) | Non-diegetic music breaks the illusion of presence |
| Strong preference for cohesion | "It was slightly jarring... because it made the environment feel slightly detached." (P7) | Mismatch between visual and auditory cues disrupts immersion |

# Limitations & Future Work
Limitations
| Limitation | Impact |
| --------------- | ----------- |
| Self-report measures (PANAS, IPQ) | Potential for social desirability bias, recall inaccuracy |
| Short-term exposure (12 minutes) | Cannot speak to long-term efficacy or therapeutic adherence |
| Single VR environment | Findings tied to specific forest scene and audio samples |
| Inability to blind participants | Distinct audio types prevented blinding (potential expectation effects) |
| Consumer hardware (Quest 2) | Findings may not generalise to higher-fidelity VR systems |

Future Work
| Direction | Rationale |
| --------------- | ----------- |
| Longitudinal studies | Assess sustained effects and adherence over days/weeks |
| Physiological measures | Heart rate variability (HRV), electrodermal activity (EDA) for objective stress measurement |
| Different environments | Beaches, mountains, urban nature – test generalisability |
| Personalised soundscapes | User-selectable or AI-adaptive audio |
| Higher-fidelity hardware | Varjo Aero, full-body tracking |
